# Human–AI Workbench: Toward a New Generation of Work Environments

Not long ago, like many others, I was surprised to discover that OpenAI had disabled Canvas. Honestly, it came as a shock, because I had used this tool extensively in my projects.

What I especially liked about it was that it functioned as a living document. I could edit it, the AI could see my changes, evaluate them, or suggest its own version. Then I would step in again, and the process could continue for a long time.

The result was usually very good. More importantly, I valued not only the result, but the process itself.

And then OpenAI took all of that away.

It was difficult and frustrating.

Once I had calmed down, I started thinking about what had happened. This is where those thoughts led me.

We can all see how rapidly AI is developing, and the main directions pursued by developers are becoming increasingly clear.

It seems that automation is currently taking center stage: enabling models to carry out sequences of actions that are less formally defined than conventional algorithms. In other words, much of the attention is focused on increasing model autonomy and creating AI assistants.

Many people welcome this development. But as a result, I lost something very important to me: I lost my intellectual partner.

I do not need a machine that buys airline tickets for me. I like choosing where I travel and where I stay.

What I need is a colleague in my work, which is often creative, investigative, or project-based. Work in which the result emerges gradually through many small and simple iterations.

Work in which either participant can introduce a new idea at any moment, redirect the search, refine a formulation, question an earlier decision, or suggest an alternative path.

Neither participant knows in advance what the final result will be.

The most important thing I realized is that the real value of this interaction lies not in dividing the labor, but in exploring the solution space together.

And I liked working with an AI in this way.

This kind of collaboration can be extremely effective because humans and AI complement each other remarkably well.

What can a human contribute?

We can retain rich and deep context. We can define goals. We have intuition and flexibility, and we can quickly shift the focus of our work.

What can AI contribute?

It can process a vast amount of information, analyze it quickly, and provide the amount of material we need. It can rapidly generate alternatives, combine information from different fields, or identify connections a human might overlook.

And, of course, it can perform routine drafting work very efficiently. “Add this idea to the document” takes the AI a second. For me, it can feel like an eternity.

Where can this kind of human–AI interaction be especially effective?

Even a quick list includes research, engineering design, software development, documentation, writing books, articles and scripts, and education.

In other words, all the areas in which the goal itself becomes clearer during the process of working toward it.

After thinking through everything above and discussing it with AI, we were able to formulate the following idea:

The problem is that we still work with AI through a “short-form” conversational interface.

At the same time, I increasingly felt the need for a **Human–AI Workbench**, or **HAI Workbench**: a unified workspace designed for long-term collaboration between humans and AI.

It is not a chat, an editor, or an agent.

It is an environment for collaborative intellectual development.

Let me emphasize this again: I am talking about a fundamentally new environment for human–AI interaction.

Its primary purpose is not to automate processes, but to develop ideas.

Not to reduce human participation, but to amplify human capabilities.

At this point, I had far more ideas than I could reasonably include here. So I will mention only some of the ones that seem most important.

First, the fundamental differences between this new environment and the tools we currently have:

* separation between the discussion space and the result space;
* one or more living documents;
* simultaneous work with multiple documents;
* the ability to include several quotations from documents and discussion history in a single request;
* discussion that does not automatically alter the documents;
* a separate mode for applying agreed changes;
* preservation of the project’s development history;
* links between documents;
* storage of ideas for later exploration.

This is only the initial level, and it could be developed much further.

For example, the group of participants could be expanded to include several people and several AI systems.

The range of objects the group works on could also be expanded. These might include diagrams, charts, decision trees, musical compositions or arrangements, images, and knowledge bases.

In fact, they could be any kind of specialized object.

Once the list of working objects expands, the idea of the result expands with it.

The result is no longer simply a document, but a system of interconnected objects: a project, a knowledge library, an engineering design—at least at the conceptual stage—or a multimedia project.

You can continue the list yourself.

Now we can look at the idea from an even broader perspective.

The HAI Workbench could be the first step toward a new generation of work environments.

We already have IDEs for software development, CAD systems for engineering design, DAWs for music production, graphics editors, video editors, collaborative document systems such as Google Docs, and many other specialized environments.

AI is already present in many of them, but only as a tool.

It seems to me that AI has become powerful enough to deserve its own environment for interacting with humans.

It is time to move beyond the limits of “chat evolution” and begin developing the tools of a new era.

Let us also be clear about what I am not proposing.

I am not proposing a specific interface. These ideas are simply a description of a unified workspace.

Nor am I proposing a particular architectural implementation.

It could be built directly into a particular AI model, developed as a separate application for that model, or created as an independent application capable of working with several AI systems—connect whichever ones you choose—through open-source or other protocols.

I am also not concerned about authorship or any other personal goals.

What matters to me is the result—and achieving that result quickly.

The more this document is discussed, the better.

The more teams begin working in this direction, the better.

With this document, I hope to communicate these ideas to as many people as possible.

Perhaps, within a few years, environments like this will become as familiar as IDEs, CAD systems, and Google Docs are today.

If this document brings their appearance even slightly closer, then writing it was worthwhile.

Working this way was very inconvenient without Canvas, but I will continue working in the way I find useful and enjoyable.
